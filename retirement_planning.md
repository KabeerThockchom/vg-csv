# Retirement Planning and Financial Management Guide

Comprehensive guide covering retirement savings, Social Security, Medicare, SECURE 2.0 Act, estate planning, healthcare costs, investment strategies, and wealth transfer.

## Question 1: What retirement savings options exist for workers without a 401(k) plan?

Workers without a 401(k) can save through traditional or Roth IRAs with $7,000 contribution limits ($8,000 for those 50+) for 2025. They can also use taxable accounts without income limits. Self-employed individuals have additional options: SEP-IRAs (up to 25% of compensation/$70,000), SIMPLE IRAs (up to $17,600 with 3% employer match), or Individual 401(k)s (up to $23,500 plus employer contributions).

## Question 2: How much can self-employed individuals contribute to a SEP-IRA for the 2025 tax year?

Self-employed individuals can contribute up to 20% of their net income reported on IRS Schedule C minus the self-employment tax to a SEP-IRA, with a maximum contribution of $70,000 for the 2025 tax year.

## Question 4: How does the SECURE 2.0 Act affect Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)?

The SECURE 2.0 Act changes RMDs in several ways: it pushes back the mandatory start date to age 73 (from 72) starting in 2023, with a further increase to age 75 by 2033. It also reduces the penalty for RMD shortfalls from 50% to 25%, with further reduction to 10% if corrected within a defined window. Additionally, a three-year statute of limitations is now in place for RMD shortfalls.

## Question 5: When should someone file for Social Security benefits to maximize their payments?

While eligible for Social Security at 62, benefits are reduced for claiming early. Full retirement age (66-67 depending on birth year) provides complete benefits. Waiting until 70 maximizes payments, with benefits increasing each month after full retirement age until age 70, potentially resulting in nearly one-third higher payments than at full retirement age. The optimal filing time depends on individual circumstances including longevity expectations, assets, and inheritance plans.

## Question 6: How does the SECURE 2.0 Act expand catch-up contribution opportunities for near-retirees?

Starting in 2025, SECURE 2.0 allows defined contribution participants aged 60-63 to make catch-up contributions of up to $10,000 annually ($5,000 for SIMPLE plans) or 50% more than the standard catch-up amount, whichever is greater. From 2024, employees earning over $145,000 must make all catch-up contributions to Roth accounts, while those earning less can continue making pre-tax catch-up contributions. Both the $10,000 and $145,000 thresholds will be indexed for inflation.

## Question 7: What options exist for converting 529 plan assets to Roth IRAs under SECURE 2.0?

Starting in 2024, SECURE 2.0 allows unused 529 plan assets to be moved to Roth IRAs if: the 529 plan is at least 15 years old; the Roth IRA is in the name of the 529 beneficiary; only funds in the 529 for at least five years can be transferred; transfers are limited to the annual Roth IRA contribution limit ($6,500 in 2023); and there's a lifetime cap of $35,000 per beneficiary. Note that 529 to Roth IRA conversions count toward annual Roth contribution limits.

## Question 8: What deadlines apply for opening different types of self-employed retirement accounts?

Self-employed individuals face different deadlines for retirement accounts: SEP-IRAs can be established and funded until the tax-filing deadline, making them more flexible. SIMPLE IRAs have stricter deadlines, generally requiring establishment by October 1 to contribute for the current tax year. Individual 401(k)s are suitable for business owners with no employees (except spouses) and offer higher contribution potential.

## Question 9: At what age can individuals withdraw from retirement accounts without penalty?

At age 59Â½, individuals can withdraw from traditional retirement accounts including IRAs and 401(k)s without being subject to the 10% early withdrawal penalty. However, withdrawals from traditional accounts will still be subject to federal income tax. Certain exceptions to the 10% penalty exist, including distributions made after death, after permanent and total disability, under a divorce agreement, or to pay unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 10% of adjusted gross income.

SECURE 2.0 expands Roth contribution opportunities starting in 2024 by allowing: small business owners to create Roth options for SIMPLE and SEP IRAs; employers to match contributions in 401(k) and 403(b) accounts with Roth dollars; and preserving the nondeductible traditional to Roth IRA conversion strategy (backdoor Roth IRA) for higher-income savers who exceed income limits for direct Roth contributions.

## Question 12: When should individuals sign up for Medicare to avoid higher premiums?

Individuals should sign up for Medicare during the 7-month initial enrollment window that starts 3 months before they turn 65. To avoid higher premiums, enrollment should occur within 3 months of their 65th birthday month. Those with employer-sponsored medical insurance can wait until that coverage ends without penalty. Planning ahead by reviewing Medicare options before turning 65 is recommended to determine the most suitable coverage.

## Question 15: What Medicare and Medigap enrollment considerations exist for 65-year-olds?

At age 65, individuals have important Medicare and Medigap enrollment decisions. The 7-month Medicare enrollment window starts 3 months before turning 65, with enrollment within 3 months of the birthday month necessary to avoid premium increases. Simultaneously, Medigap enrollment begins the first day of the 65th birthday month, offering supplemental private insurance covering costs not covered by traditional Medicare. During this enrollment window, applicants cannot be denied Medigap coverage or charged extra due to health conditions. Those with employer insurance can postpone both Medicare and Medigap enrollment without penalty until that coverage ends.

## Question 16: What are the two main ways to access retirement money before age 59Â½ without penalties?

The two main ways to access retirement funds early without triggering the 10% early withdrawal penalty are Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPPs) and the Rule of 55. SEPPs is a withdrawal option starting before age 59Â½ and lasting either until age 59Â½ or 5 years, whichever is later. The Rule of 55 allows those who leave their employer on or after the year they turn 55 to begin taking withdrawals from their 401(k) or 403(b) from that employer without penalties.

## Question 17: How do the three SEPP calculation methods differ in terms of withdrawal amounts?

There are three SEPP calculation methods that yield different withdrawal amounts: 1) Fixed amortization usually results in the highest withdrawal amount and remains the same in future years, 2) Required minimum distribution typically provides the lowest withdrawal amount and is simplest to calculate, and 3) Fixed annuitization usually results in an amount somewhere in the middle and remains consistent annually.

## Question 18: What are the life expectancy table options for calculating SEPPs and how do they affect withdrawal amounts?

When calculating SEPPs, you can choose from three life expectancy tables that affect your withdrawal amounts: 1) The single life expectancy table usually results in the highest withdrawal amount, 2) The uniform life table typically yields the lowest withdrawal amount, and 3) The joint life and last survivor table generally provides an amount in the middle unless the beneficiary is more than 10 years younger than the owner, then it becomes the lowest withdrawal amount.

## Question 20: What behavioral factors make it difficult for retirees to spend their retirement savings?

Three behavioral factors make it difficult for retirees to spend their retirement savings: 1) Self-control: Farsighted investors who exercised great discipline in saving may continue this pattern in retirement, feeling regret for not spending more on life's pleasures; 2) Loss aversion: Retirees experience negative feelings when withdrawing money as it feels like a loss, making retirement withdrawals emotionally difficult; 3) Scarcity and opportunity costs: Spending from what seems like a fixed pool of nonreplenishing resources creates stress about trade-offs and future needs, even for those with sufficient funds.

## Question 21: How can retirees overcome the psychological barrier of loss aversion when making retirement withdrawals?

Retirees can overcome loss aversion when making retirement withdrawals through two main strategies: 1) Change their mindset by considering monthly or annual withdrawals as paychecks to themselves rather than money being taken away from savings, and 2) Consider taking larger distributions less frequently (quarterly, biannual, or annual basis) instead of smaller monthly withdrawals, setting the larger amount aside in a separate spending account. These approaches help reduce the negative feelings associated with multiple withdrawal "losses."

## Question 23: What are the six key factors affecting healthcare costs in retirement?

The six key factors affecting healthcare costs in retirement are: 1) Medicare choice: The plan you select affects coverage and costs; 2) Health status: Your health when entering retirement and family health history impact needed coverage; 3) Retirement age: Early retirement requires funding healthcare before Medicare eligibility; 4) Employer subsidy: Losing employer subsidies can increase costs by an average of $6,440; 5) Location: While traditional Medicare costs are consistent nationwide, other expenses like prescription coverage vary by location; and 6) Income in retirement: Higher income may trigger Medicare surcharges (over $103,000 for individuals or $206,000 for married couples in 2024).

## Question 38: How do Roth IRAs differ from traditional IRAs in terms of tax benefits?

Roth IRAs and traditional IRAs differ primarily in their tax treatment.
